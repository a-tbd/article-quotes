    Live Analysis: Oral Arguments on Trump’s Immigration Order

    By CHARLIE SAVAGE, JULIE HIRSCHFELD DAVIS and ERIC LICHTBLAU FEB. 7, 2017

    1. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      5:45 PM ET

      Hi, everyone.

      President Trump gets the first big legal test of his young administration this evening, as the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals, based in San Francisco, hears arguments over the president’s targeted travel ban, four days after a federal judge in Seattle blocked the plan.

      Three appellate judges from the circuit court will hear arguments for an hour via conference call from the Justice Department and the state of Washington, which challenged the plan as illegal and unconstitutional.

      No word on whether Mr. Trump — who berated the “so-called judge” for his “ridiculous” decision — will be listening in on the arguments, but we certainly will.

      I’ll be joined by two other reporters in The Times’s Washington bureau — Julie Hirschfeld Davis and Charlie Savage — with the legal play-by-play.

    2. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      5:45 PM ET

      For some background on everything you need to know about the legal issues surrounding the travel restrictions, check out a piece by my colleague Adam Liptak, our reporter at the Supreme Court — which is probably where this case will end up no matter who wins this round of arguments:

      The Travel Ban Court Argument: What You Need to Know

      WASHINGTON - The federal appeals court in San Francisco will hear arguments on Tuesday in a challenge to President Trump’s targeted travel ban. Here’s what you need to know. When is the hearing and how can I listen? The court has scheduled an hour of arguments to start at 3 p.m.

      The New York Times

    3. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      5:54 PM ET

      The president said on Tuesday that he hoped the matter would not have to go all the way to the Supreme Court, but that defending his order was “very important for the country.”

      His press secretary, Sean Spicer, said the administration felt “confident” that it will ultimately prevail on the merits of this case, citing laws that give the president broad discretion over admitting immigrants.

      He put a little pre-emptive spin on tonight’s proceedings to play down their significance, emphasizing that the question at hand was only about whether to lift the restraining order blocking Mr. Trump’s directive — not the substance of the policy.

    4. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      5:58 PM ET

      Two of the justices who will be hearing the arguments were appointed by a Democrat, and the third was named by a Republican. Circuit Judge Michelle T. Friedland, who sits in San Jose and is presiding over the hearing, was appointed by President Barack Obama, Senior Circuit Judge William C. Canby, Jr., in Phoenix, was appointed by President Jimmy Carter, and Senior Circuit Judge Richard R. Clifton in Honolulu, was appointed by President George W. Bush.

    5. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:02 PM ET

      The hearing just started, with the clerk making sure everyone is on the line. So we’re all over the map, with judges and lawyers in Seattle, Washington D.C., San Jose, Pheonix and Hawaii.

    7. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:04 PM ET

      Judge Michelle Friedland opens. She’s an Obama appointee, and a former clerk to Justice Sandra Day O'Connor.

    8. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:05 PM ET

      Arguing for the government, August E. Flentje, Special Counsel to the Assistant U.S. Attorney General in Washington, opens by citing previous risk assessments done before Trump was in office. He says the travel order was “well within the president’s power” and is constitutional.

    9. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:06 PM ET

      Flentje says the order was well within the president’s power as delegated by Congress and it is constitutional. He cites the Federal District Court judge in Boston who let the temporary restraining order in Massachusetts expire in a ruling on Friday.

    10. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:07 PM ET

      Judge Canby interrupts to clarify that this is about whether Temporary Restraining Order standards have been met, not about the substance of the underlying legal dispute.

    11. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:08 PM ET

      Flentje is citing an argument the president’s team has been making from the beginning when criticized on this order — it was Obama who chose these seven countries, not Trump. While it is true that the list of countries corresponds to one Obama made that would require visas, he never sought to bar entrants from those countries. It’s a big difference.

    12. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:08 PM ET

      Flentje says blocking the immigration order would cause “irreparable harm,” but Friedland sounds skeptical and asks what evidence they have.

    13. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:10 PM ET

      Judge Friedland asks whether the government has pointed to any evidence connecting these seven countries to terrorism. Flentje again points to prior determinations by Congress that these are countries of concern based on “significant terrorist activity in the country.”

    14. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:11 PM ET

      Judge Clifton notes that there were already procedures in place. Is there any reason to think something has changed to create a real risk?

    15. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:11 PM ET

      Flentje says the president determined there was a real risk.

    16. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:11 PM ET

      Justice Clifton sounds skeptical, too, saying that the administration’s assessment of the risks posed by immigrants from the seven countries sounds “pretty abstract.”

    17. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:12 PM ET

      Judge Clifton’s line of questioning points to an irony in the Trump administration’s defense of this order: Trump is essentially arguing that this order is needed because Obama was too lax, but in doing so, he is relying on an action Obama took to substantially toughen procedures for people from these countries.

    18. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:12 PM ET

      Judge Canby notes that in naming those seven countries, Congress determined that people coming in from them had to have visas, so that “permitted the usual investigations before you give someone a visa.” He notes that there is no evidence of terrorist activity by anyone who came in on such a visa.

    19. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:13 PM ET

      Canby asks how many offenses were committed by people from those seven countries. He then answers his own question, saying there apparently have been none.

    20. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:13 PM ET

      Flentje notes that “there have been a number of people from Somalia connected to Al Shabab” convicted of terrorism crimes. But he acknowledges that these are not in the record before the court.

    21. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:14 PM ET

      Flentje keeps saying that this has all been litigated very quickly, but Judge Friedland points out that it was the government that asked for an emergency appeal. She sounds annoyed that the Justice Department wants it both ways.

    22. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:15 PM ET

      Flentje stresses that it is the president who is charged with making decisions about risks to national security. Judge Friedland asks whether he is saying that the president’s decisions are unreviewable. He says yes — she presses him for what the constitutional limits to that may be.

    24. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:16 PM ET

      Flentje stresses congressional delegation of broad authority to the president about excluding classes of foreigners from the country. This is an exercise of sovereignty shared by Congress and the president — i.e., stay out of it, judiciary!

    25. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:16 PM ET

      Charlie, it sounds to me that all three of the judges sound pretty skeptical of the government’s position. What do you think?

    26. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:17 PM ET

      It’s always risky to try to guess where judges are going, but I tentatively agree Eric. This is a hot bench.

    27. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:18 PM ET

      Yes, they sound somewhat irritated and I find myself really wishing there were video of this.

    28. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:18 PM ET

      Judge Friedland just threw in a land mine — whether allegations of “bad faith” should be considered. It’s not exactly clear to me what she’s referring to, but she may be hinting at accusations from petitioners that the government acted way too quickly without a basic plan for implementing the order.

    29. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:19 PM ET

      All three judges are now jumping on Flentje’s legal argument simultaneously — talking over one another. Not good for President Trump.

    30. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:20 PM ET

      I have no information at the moment about whether or how Trump is following this, but he likes to watch TV and often watches cable news in the evening. If he does now, he will see only head shots of the speakers and hear the same live feed we are.

    31. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:20 PM ET

      If he’s listening, Julie, he can’t like the way this is going.

    32. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:21 PM ET

      There’s a long pause from Flentje in answering a question from one of the justices about who has standing to sue the government here.

    33. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:22 PM ET

      Judge Clifton, the sole Republican appointee, sounds skeptical of the Trump administration’s argument that the state of Washington doesn’t have standing to challenge the order. The state has a proprietary interest as owner and operator of universities that can’t bring in foreign speakers or students.

    34. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:23 PM ET

      If this were Sunday night’s Super Bowl, the Patriots would be down 21-0. But let’s not forget how that turned out.

    35. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:25 PM ET

      Judge Canby wants to know whether the president could issue an order that simply says, “We’re not going to let any Muslims in.” Flentje tries to evade it, but Judge Clifton wants to know, too.

    36. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:25 PM ET

      Canby: “Could the president simply say in the order, ‘We’re not going to let any Muslims in’?”

    37. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:25 PM ET

      Flentje: “This is a far cry from that situation.”

    38. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:26 PM ET

      “It’s not an order that discriminates on the basis of religion,” so there would not be a valid claim under equal protection, Flentje says. But he won’t answer on whether Washington State would have standing to challenge an order that did that.

    39. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:26 PM ET

      Flentje acknowledges there are other cases where standing arguments are strong. But in this case, he says, there are a lot of laws saying states can’t step into citizens’ shoes in this context.

    40. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:27 PM ET

      Flentje is basically arguing that this is the wrong lawsuit to test the order. Other claims, brought by individuals blocked from coming into the country, would be better to litigate, he says.

    41. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:27 PM ET

      “I’m not sure I’m convincing the court,” Flentje says in a bit of understatement.

    42. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:27 PM ET

      Flentje: Even if the court thinks some applications of the executive order are problematic, the restraining order is overly broad.

    43. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:28 PM ET

      Friedland: The states are claiming that the executive order violates the Establishment Clause. If that’s true, wouldn’t it be invalid on its face?

    44. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:30 PM ET

      Flentje says this court should immediately stay the injunction as to people who aren’t inside the United States and don’t have ties to the country yet.

    46. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:30 PM ET

      Flentje’s point is that non-citizens abroad who don’t have ties to the U.S. do not have constitutional rights.

    47. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:30 PM ET

      Now Noah Purcell, the solicitor general for the state of Washington, is arguing.

    48. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:31 PM ET

      Purcell says the government is asking the court to “throw this country back into chaos.”

    49. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:33 PM ET

      Judge Clifton jumps right in — “Why should we care?” — on Purcell’s argument that the Justice Department is pursuing the wrong remedy.

    50. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:33 PM ET

      A hot bench indeed.

    51. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:34 PM ET

      Purcell says the government has delayed this whole process by bringing the emergency appeal to the appellate court rather than continuing in the district court.

    52. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:34 PM ET

      Judge Clifton is pushing Purcell not to focus on whether temporary restraining order review standards have been met. Since the District Court has already decided this temporary restraining order is going to stay in place longer than 14 days, maybe the court should look at this as if it were a preliminary injunction.

    53. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:35 PM ET

      The point is that it’s relatively easy to get a temporary restraining order. It’s harder to justify an injunction.

    54. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:36 PM ET

      There is no “irreparable harm” to the Trump administraiton here, Purcell says. It was the order itself that caused harm to people in the state of Washington, he says.

    55. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:36 PM ET

      “It was the executive order itself that caused irreparable harm to our states” and many people, Purcell says, and he’s now listing them.

      Correspondent

      6:36 PM ET

      Purcell: We had students and faculty stranded overseas, families separated, lost tax revenue…

    57. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:37 PM ET

      He’s clearly trying to put a human face on the harm done by the order and move away from the legalese that came before.

    58. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:38 PM ET

      The “parens patriae” theory they keep mentioning is the idea that the government is the legal protector of people who are unable to protect themselves, which would give the state of Washington standing to bring this challenge.

    59. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:38 PM ET

      “At this point, it’s really the federal government that’s asking the court to upset the status quo,” Purcell argues, saying that things are slowly returning to normal after the chaos at airports that followed the issuance of this order.

    60. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:40 PM ET

      The judges are challenging Purcell on a few points about legal process, but not nearly as skeptically as they did with the Justice Department. If I’m scoring at home, I’d say the Patriots (Trump) are still down 28-9.

    61. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:41 PM ET

      Purcell is suggesting that the Justice Department’s arguments are in bad faith — that the Trump administration has a different motive from what it is arguing.

    62. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:41 PM ET

      How many people affected are from Washington State, Judge Canby wants to know. Purcell says that it’s too early to know for sure, but that ‘thousands’ could be affected.

    63. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:41 PM ET

      The panel also hasn’t stumped Purcell in quite the same way they did Flentje, who had several agonizingly long pauses trying to answer their questions.

    64. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:43 PM ET

      They are focusing now on lawful permanent residents (green-card holders). The Trump order initially barred them from re-entering the country, but the White House later backed off. Are they still relevant?

    65. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:43 PM ET

      Purcell is arguing for why the 9th Circuit should not limit the temporary restraining order to lawful permanent residents.

    66. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:44 PM ET

      He lists other harms beyond green-card holders.

    68. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:45 PM ET

      Purcell says the Trump administration has “changed their minds about five times” about whether the executive order applies to green card holders from the seven countries. (He says there are a half a million of them).

      That’s an exaggeration, but he’s right that there was massive confusion from the White House on down about whether it applied or not.

      Trump’s closest aides initially said unequivocally that it did apply to legal permanent residents. Then it wasn’t clear. And now John Kelly, the secretary of homeland security, has said definitively it does not.

    69. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:45 PM ET

      Purcell says the government doesn’t have a clear plan for how it would make sure permanent legal residents can make it in.

    70. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:45 PM ET

      He says this is clearly targeted at a religious group: Muslims.

    71. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:46 PM ET

      They are now discussing whether the order amounts to unconstitutional religious discrimination. Judge Clifton notes that the seven countries amount to less than 15 percent of Muslims worldwide.

    72. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:47 PM ET

      Clifton: “I have trouble understanding why we are supposed to infer religious animus when in fact the vast majority of Muslims would not be affected as residents of those seven countries” and where the connection to terrorism for those seven countries “is kind of hard to deny.”

    73. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:48 PM ET

      He says he has “trouble understanding” how this is “religious animus” if most Muslims overseas wouldn’t be affected.

    74. Eric Lichtblau

      Correspondent

      6:48 PM ET

      Purcell cites “shocking evidence of intent to discriminate against Muslims” — including public statements from Trump himself and his aides.

    75. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:48 PM ET

      A reminder about the seven countries in question here: Iran, Iraq, Libya, Somalia, Sudan, Syria and Yemen.

    76. Charlie Savage

      Correspondent

      6:48 PM ET

      Purcell cites public statements from Trump and his top advisers as sufficient evidence at pleading stage to go forward. He hints that if they get to discovery they may find more stuff in private communications among government officials.

    77. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

      6:49 PM ET

    78. Eric Lichtblau

      6:49 PM ET

      This is the strongest challenge that Purcell and the petitioners have faced in the hearing.

    79. Charlie Savage

    80. Julie Hirschfeld Davis

      White House Correspondent

    81. Eric Lichtblau

      6:50 PM ET

      “We’re not denying that these countries could have theoretically” been designated a risk, Purcell says.
